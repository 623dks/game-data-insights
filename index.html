Introduction

The problem with getting advice

▼

Steam has 200,000+ games. Anyone can ship. But there's no real roadmap for success — just scattered tools and conflicting advice. So we asked: what does the data actually say?

⚠️

The information gap

Current tools show you what players thought about one game. They don't show patterns across thousands of games, or what separates successful releases from failed ones.

💡

The data-first approach

By analyzing games at scale, patterns emerge: what successful games share, what struggling games have in common, and what that means for real development decisions.

🎯

Who this helps

Indie developers, art directors, marketing teams, and platforms like Steam itself, where better pattern analysis means better recommendations for players.

Research Questions
  • 01Do indie games perform differently from AAA titles in terms of user feedback?
  • 02Can promotional materials like descriptions and screenshots predict a game's success?
  • 03How do game trailers affect user reviews and ratings?
  • 04Do certain genres consistently score better in user ratings?
  • 05How do regional preferences shape the success of specific genres?
  • 06Is trailer length correlated with user ratings?
  • 07How does social media activity influence a game's success?
  • 08How do reviews change over time after a game's release?
  • 09Are there musical genres commonly found in highly-rated games?
  • 10What compositional elements in soundtracks correlate with positive reviews?
Data Collection and Exploration

Building the dataset

▼

All data came directly from Steam's public API. No third-party datasets, no scraping workarounds.

Three endpoints, millions of records

The pipeline starts by fetching all app IDs, then loops through each one to pull full app metadata and paginated reviews. Cursor-based pagination handles multi-page review sets.

All responses come back as JSON. From there, data gets cleaned and organized into CSV files using a consistent schema, with one exception: the description column needed additional processing to strip HTML tags and non-text characters.

// Three API endpoints powering the dataset

GET api.steampowered.com/ISteamApps/GetAppList/v2/
All Steam app IDs

GET store.steampowered.com/api/appdetails?appids=<ID>
Full metadata for a single app

GET store.steampowered.com/appreviews/<ID>?json=1
&num_per_page=100&cursor=<CUR>
Paginated reviews per app

Data Cleaning

Getting reviews into shape

The tricky part of cleaning is the description field, which comes filled with HTML tags, special characters, and noise. Reviews and descriptions were also transformed into transaction format — each row is a document and each column a word token. This structure is required input for Association Rule Mining.

Models Implemented

Three models. One question.

▼

We combined unsupervised language mining, vision-language alignment, and gradient-boosted image feature modeling to cover the full range of game signals.

Model 01Association Rule Mining

Finding patterns in what players write

ARM looks at which words appear together in game reviews, grouped by genre and by whether the game was mostly liked or mostly disliked. Using the Apriori algorithm, it surfaces which concepts cluster together and how strongly. Games were labeled positive or negative based on their overall review ratio. Reviews were stripped down to meaningful tokens, removing punctuation, numbers, stopwords, duplicates, and non-latin characters. Basket data was generated per genre across all 10 genres. Positive reviews: support 0.01, confidence 0.4. Negative reviews: support 0.02, confidence 0.55.

Model 02CLIP — Vision-Language Alignment

Connecting game images to words

CLIP maps images and text into a shared embedding space. We used it to automatically tag game screenshots with genre and context labels — no fine-tuning required. The architecture runs a ViT for images and a Transformer for text, comparing outputs using cosine similarity. For novel pairs, a score above 0.2 is considered meaningful alignment.

Before and After Cleaning

Before. Raw game descriptions — irrelevant tokens and 404-error image URLs.

After. Cleaned descriptions after stripping noise. Cleaner text → better embeddings.

🔍

What this opens up for developers

Drop a screenshot, get genre tags automatically. Find comparable successful games, gather inspiration faster than manual research, and feed better signals into recommendation systems.

Model 03Gradient Boosting — Image Features

Do visual properties predict success?

Gradient Boosting builds models sequentially, each new tree correcting errors from the last. We predict game success — measured by positive review ratio — from per-image visual features: brightness, saturation, hue, and contrast extracted from screenshots. MSE of 130 confirms the model learns this relationship effectively.

Dataset Creation

Feature extraction. Each row = one game, with extracted visual metrics and review ratio as prediction target.

Model Performance

MSE = 130. The model learns the relationship between image features and review ratios without overfitting.

Feature Analysis

Correlation heatmap. Most features are independent. One exception: contrast + brightness share moderate correlation.

Saturation vs. success. More saturated images predict lower review ratios. Garish visuals signal lower production quality.

Contrast vs. success. Sweet spot: 50–80. Too low feels flat; too high feels harsh. Both hurt scores.

Hue vs. success. Color choice alone doesn't predict success. Slight edge for greens/yellows, nothing conclusive.

Brightness vs. success. No clear correlation. Brightness is a free variable for developers.

🎨

Practical takeaways for art direction

Target contrast between 50 and 80 in marketing screenshots. Pull back on saturation. Clean, controlled color palettes outperform maximalist ones. These are data-backed guidelines, not aesthetic opinion.

Conclusions

What we found

▼
📖

Story is the spine

Across genres, story emerged as the central cluster in positive reviews. A weak story undermines everything built on top of it.

🎵

Good craft disappears

In well-received games, music, visuals, and mechanics support the experience rather than competing for attention.

🎮

Restrictions kill engagement

In multiplayer games, restricted player freedom showed up clearly as a failure driver. Players feel when they're boxed in.

Video games are interactive art. They bring together narrative design, visuals, music, sound, and mechanical systems into a single coherent experience. Our results showed that coherence, and especially a quality story, is one of the most consistently appreciated elements across genres.

Does story outrank everything else? We'd say no. Without strong supporting elements, even a good narrative falls flat. Take the massively multiplayer genre: in negatively reviewed games, the dominant theme in player language was limitation. Players couldn't do what they wanted. The game itself had become the obstacle.

On the visual side, we showed the potential to automatically translate a game screenshot into a genre and context label, with no fine-tuning required. The ability to auto-tag a static image means a developer can find games with similar visual styles much faster.

We also found a quantifiable link between image properties and game success. Contrast and saturation carry real signal: an optimal contrast range of 50 to 80 predicts better-performing games, and higher saturation correlates negatively with success.

Reviews and screenshots are just one slice of what shapes a game. Music, sound design, lighting, story structure, and platform-specific behavior patterns all remain largely unexplored. The methodology here scales, and so does the potential for what comes next.
